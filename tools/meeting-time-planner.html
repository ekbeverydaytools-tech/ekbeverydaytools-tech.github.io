    How this works

    Pick a reference date and two to four time zones. The planner takes each hour of that day in the first zone you list and asks your browser what the local wall-clock time is in every other zone at that exact moment. Those conversions use the Intl.DateTimeFormat engine built into every modern browser, which ships with the full IANA time-zone database — the same data your operating system relies on. That means daylight-saving switches, half-hour offsets like India’s, and future rule changes are handled automatically; nothing here is approximated with fixed UTC offset tables.

    Each cell is coloured by how sociable that hour is locally: green for 9:00–17:00, amber for the shoulder hours (8:00–9:00 and 17:00–19:00), and grey for everything else. When a whole column lands in green for every zone, it is outlined as a best time and listed in plain English below the grid, ready to paste into an email or chat with the copy button.

    Because everything runs locally in your browser, the tool works offline once loaded and never transmits the zones or dates you enter. If a cell shows +1 or −1, that hour falls on the next or previous calendar day in that zone — worth double-checking before you send the invite.

    Frequently asked questions

    Does this handle daylight saving time correctly?

    Yes. All conversions come from your browser’s built-in IANA time-zone database via the Intl API, so DST transitions are applied per zone and per date. Pick a date in March or November and you will see offsets shift exactly when they do in real life — no fixed offset tables are used.

    What counts as a “best time”?

    An hour is marked as a best time when the local clock reads between 9:00 and 16:59 in every zone you selected — standard 9-to-5 working hours everywhere at once. Amber cells (8–9 am and 5–7 pm) are reasonable fallbacks when no green overlap exists.

    Can I plan a meeting for a future date?

    Yes. Change the reference date to any day you like. Because DST rules differ by date, the overlap between, say, New York and London is 5 hours in July but can be 4 hours for a couple of weeks in March — the grid reflects that automatically.

    Why do some cells show +1 or −1?

    The columns follow one calendar day in your first zone. Zones far to the east or west may already be on the next day (+1) or still on the previous day (−1) at that moment. The marker warns you that the meeting date differs in that zone.

    Is anything I enter sent to a server?

    No. The page is fully client-side: no analytics, no network requests, no accounts. Your zones and dates never leave your device, and the tool keeps working offline once the page is loaded.
