How this works

This calculator works backwards from the life you want to fund. Start with the take-home pay you need after taxes, then add your annual business costs — software, insurance, equipment, accounting. Because taxes come off the top of everything you earn, that combined figure is divided by (1 − your effective tax rate) to find the gross revenue your business must bring in.

Next it counts the hours you can actually bill. Freelancers rarely invoice 40 hours a week: admin, marketing, proposals, and email eat real time, and 20–30 billable hours is a realistic ceiling for most people. Weekly billable hours multiplied by working weeks (52 minus your time off) gives your billable hours per year. Divide gross revenue by those hours and you have a break-even hourly rate; the safety buffer on top absorbs late payments, quiet months, and projects that run long.

The day rate assumes an eight-hour billable day, and the monthly retainer spreads your full annual capacity across twelve months — a useful anchor when a client wants ongoing work. Treat every number here as a floor, not a ceiling: when the value you deliver justifies more, charge more.

Frequently asked questions

Why can't I just divide my target salary by 2,080 hours?

Employees get 2,080 paid hours a year; freelancers don't. You lose hours to admin, marketing, proposals, and email, you take unpaid time off, you cover your own expenses, and taxes come out of gross revenue. Dividing a salary by 2,080 produces a rate that quietly underpays you — often by 40% or more.

What effective tax rate should I enter?

Enter the total percentage of gross income you expect to pay across all taxes — income tax, self-employment or social contributions, and state or local taxes — after deductions. For many US freelancers this lands between 25% and 35%. If unsure, check last year's return: total tax paid divided by gross income.

What counts as a billable hour?

Only time you can actually invoice a client for. Admin, bookkeeping, marketing, sales calls, and learning don't count. Most full-time freelancers bill 50–60% of their working time, so 20–30 billable hours per week is a realistic ceiling even for a 40-hour week.

What is the safety buffer for?

The buffer inflates your rate to absorb the risks of freelancing: late invoices, dry spells between projects, scope creep, and work that takes longer than quoted. 10% is a sensible minimum; 15–20% is wise if your income is lumpy or clients pay slowly.

Is my financial data stored or sent anywhere?

No. This calculator runs entirely in your browser with plain JavaScript. Nothing you type is saved, transmitted, or tracked — you can even use it offline once the page has loaded.
