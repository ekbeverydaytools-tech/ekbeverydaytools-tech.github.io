How this works

The calculator first derives your standard monthly payment with the amortization formula: balance × monthly rate ÷ (1 − (1 + monthly rate)−months), where the monthly rate is APR ÷ 12. For example, $250,000 at 6.5% APR with 25 years left works out to about $1,688 per month.

It then simulates the loan month by month, twice. Each simulated month, interest equals the remaining balance times the monthly rate; whatever is left of your payment reduces principal. The baseline run uses only the standard payment, the second run adds your extra amount. Because every extra dollar goes straight to principal, all future months accrue interest on a smaller balance — that compounding in reverse is why a modest extra payment removes whole years from the end of the loan, where payments would have been mostly interest already paid off.

The results compare the two runs directly: months to payoff, total interest, and the gap between them. The math assumes a fixed rate, monthly compounding, and no fees, and that your lender applies extra amounts to principal immediately — worth confirming, since some lenders credit them to the next payment instead unless you ask.

Frequently asked questions

Does paying a little extra each month really make a difference?

Yes, and it is larger than most people expect. On a $250,000 loan at 6.5% with 25 years left, an extra $200 a month clears the loan roughly 5½ years early and saves around $65,000 in interest, because every extra dollar reduces the balance that all future interest is charged on.

Is it better to pay extra monthly or make one lump-sum payment?

Dollar for dollar, money applied sooner saves more, so a lump sum today beats the same total spread over years. But a steady monthly extra is easier to sustain and still front-loads most of the benefit. You can approximate a lump sum here by reducing the current balance input.

Does this work for mortgages, car loans, and student loans?

It works for any fixed-rate, monthly-compounding amortized loan, which covers most mortgages, auto loans, personal loans, and many student loans. It does not model variable rates, interest-only periods, or credit card minimum payments.

Do I need to tell my lender to apply extra payments to principal?

Often, yes. Some lenders treat unlabeled overpayments as an early next payment or hold them in suspense, which saves you nothing. Mark extra amounts as "apply to principal" and check your next statement to confirm the balance dropped by the full extra amount.

Why is my lender's payoff quote slightly different from this calculator?

Lenders often accrue interest daily rather than monthly, and payoff quotes include per-diem interest to a specific date, plus any escrow or fees. Differences of a few dollars to a few hundred are normal; the payoff timing and interest savings shown here remain accurate guides.
